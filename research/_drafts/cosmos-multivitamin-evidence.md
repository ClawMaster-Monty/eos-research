# What COSMOS Found About Multivitamins and Cognition

**EOS Research — Review draft, not for publication**  
**Evidence Confidence:** 🔵 Moderate Confidence — A small cognitive-test effect was detected  
**Evidence cutoff:** 19 July 2026

> **Editorial disclaimer:** This review is educational and is not medical advice. COSMOS tested one specific commercial multivitamin-mineral formulation in generally healthy older US volunteers. Its findings should not be generalized automatically to other formulations, doses, younger adults, people with diagnosed deficiencies, or dementia treatment.

## The bottom line

COSMOS found small average differences on selected cognitive tests among older adults. Across its pooled cognitive studies, daily multivitamin-mineral assignment was associated with differences in change of **0.07 standard-deviation (SD) units in global cognition** and **0.06 SD units in episodic memory**. SD is a standardized test-score scale, not a percentage reduction in decline. Both pooled outcomes passed the meta-analysis paper’s two-test Bonferroni threshold.[C06]

That is not the same as demonstrating that multivitamins prevent dementia, preserve independence, or delay cognitive decline by two years. COSMOS-Mind separately assessed incident mild cognitive impairment and probable dementia. It found **no statistically significant reduction in either outcome**, with only 14 probable-dementia cases and limited statistical power.[C04]

## The question

How large and clinically meaningful were the cognitive differences—and what, if anything, do they tell us about dementia risk or everyday function?

Answering that requires separating five things that are often blended together:

1. the parent trial’s prespecified cancer and cardiovascular endpoints;
2. individual cognitive substudy results;
3. the pooled cognitive analysis;
4. a separate MCI and probable-dementia incidence analysis;
5. derived “years of cognitive aging” translations.

## What COSMOS was designed to test

COSMOS randomized **21,442 US adults** in a double-blind 2×2 factorial trial of cocoa extract, a daily multivitamin-mineral, both, or placebo. Women entered at age 65 or older and men at age 60 or older. Participants completed a placebo run-in and had to demonstrate at least 75% pill adherence before randomization.[C01,C02,C07]

For the multivitamin intervention, the parent trial’s primary endpoint was **total invasive cancer**, not cognition; total cardiovascular disease and mortality were secondary outcomes. None showed a statistically significant reduction over a median 3.6 years.[C02] The cognition findings came from prespecified ancillary studies within this parent trial. That does not invalidate them, but it defines their place in the evidence hierarchy.

## What the cognition studies found

### COSMOS-Mind

COSMOS-Mind enrolled **2,262 participants** and assessed cognition by telephone at baseline and annually for three years.[C03]

The study’s primary intervention comparison was cocoa extract on global cognition; it was null: **0.03 SD, 95% CI −0.02 to 0.08; p=0.28**. The multivitamin-mineral (MVM) comparison was prespecified as a key secondary endpoint and favored MVM assignment: **0.07 SD, 95% CI 0.02–0.12; p=0.007**.[C03]

The MVM comparison was a prespecified key secondary endpoint, but the paper did not control type-I error across its secondary and tertiary comparisons; memory, executive-function, and subgroup findings were likewise unadjusted.[C03]

### COSMOS-Web

COSMOS-Web randomized **3,960 participants**; **3,562 with baseline and at least one follow-up assessment** comprised the intention-to-treat analysis sample. Its prespecified primary endpoint was one-year immediate word recall—not global cognition.[C05]

The modeled treatment difference in change was **0.23 recalled words**, standardized effect **d=0.070**, with p=0.025. The average difference in change across three years was **0.15 words**, d=0.048, p=0.011. Secondary retention, object-recognition, and executive-function outcomes were not significant.[C05]

This was a statistically positive result on one memory task with a small standardized effect. Describing Web as proof of broad global-cognition improvement overstates what it measured.

### COSMOS-Clinic

COSMOS-Clinic included **573 participants** with baseline in-person testing; 492 completed all two-year tests.[C06]

Its primary global-cognition difference in change was **0.06 SD, 95% CI −0.003 to 0.13**. Because the confidence interval crossed zero, Clinic did not independently meet its global-cognition primary endpoint. A secondary episodic-memory difference in change favored multivitamins: **0.12 SD, 95% CI 0.002–0.23**. Secondary outcomes were not adjusted for multiple comparisons.[C06]

### The pooled analysis

The later within-COSMOS meta-analysis used nonoverlapping participants from Mind, Web, and Clinic, but the sample differed by outcome:[C06]

| Pooled outcome | Participants | Effect estimate | Statistical result |
|---|---:|---:|---:|
| Global cognition | 2,731 | 0.07-SD difference in change | 95% CI 0.03–0.11; p=0.0009 |
| Episodic memory | 5,203 | 0.06-SD difference in change | 95% CI 0.03–0.10; p=0.0007 |

Web did not contribute to global cognition because it did not measure a corresponding prespecified global-cognition endpoint. The pooled paper treated global cognition and episodic memory as two coprimary outcomes and applied a Bonferroni threshold of 0.025; both passed.[C06]

These were small differences from studies nested within one parent trial, not results from three independent trials. Even with nonoverlapping samples, common eligibility, intervention, infrastructure, and investigators can create shared biases or correlated context; the pooled analysis cannot prove how one participant’s trajectory compares across all three testing modes.

## MCI and dementia outcomes

No significant prevention effect was demonstrated.

COSMOS-Mind prespecified incident mild cognitive impairment and probable all-cause dementia as secondary aims. A masked panel adjudicated **110 incident MCI cases and 14 incident probable-dementia cases** over three years.[C04]

| Outcome | Multivitamin vs placebo |
|---|---:|
| Incident MCI | HR 0.91; 95% CI 0.63–1.32; p=0.62 |
| Incident probable dementia | HR 0.76; 95% CI 0.27–2.20; p=0.62 |

The study estimated only **22% power** to detect a 20% MCI effect; approximately 631 MCI cases would have been required for 80% power.[C04] The null results therefore do not prove that no clinically meaningful effect exists. They do mean COSMOS did not establish one.

A post hoc analysis found more favorable test-score trajectories among multivitamin-assigned participants who converted to MCI. Because it conditions on a post-randomization event and was exploratory, it cannot override the null incidence comparison or establish that multivitamins prevented conversion.[C04]

## What “two years of cognitive aging” means

The widely repeated phrase was not a directly measured delay in aging.

The pooled paper translated its **0.07-SD global-cognition effect** by comparing it with the cross-sectional score difference associated with participants being approximately two years apart in age.[C06] Related COSMOS papers produced different translations from different tests and age slopes:

- COSMOS-Mind: approximately **1.8 years**;
- COSMOS-Web: approximately **3.1 years**;
- COSMOS-Clinic episodic memory: approximately **4.8 years**.[C03,C05,C06]

These calculations can help communicate scale, but they are model-dependent. They do not show:

- two longitudinal years of decline prevented;
- a two-year delay in MCI or dementia;
- slower biological aging;
- preserved activities of daily living;
- two additional years of independent living.

The most precise wording is: **“equivalent to the cross-sectional score difference associated with roughly two years of age.”** That is not the same as a measured two-year delay in decline.

## What the broader evidence adds

### An earlier long-term trial did not show a cognitive benefit

Physicians' Health Study II randomized 5,947 male physicians aged 65 or older and found no multivitamin benefit for global cognitive change (−0.01 SD, 95% CI −0.04 to 0.02) or verbal-memory change (−0.005 SD, 95% CI −0.04 to 0.03). The final assessment occurred an average 8.5 years after randomization, but the study lacked a prerandomization cognitive baseline. It is informative counterevidence, not a like-for-like replication of COSMOS.[C14]

### Biomarkers show exposure, not a proven cognitive mechanism

In a randomized 399-person substudy, multivitamin assignment increased selected serum carotenoids and α-tocopherol over two years. A 958-person epigenetic-clock analysis reported nominal changes in two of five clocks, but neither survived correction across the five tests; a separate 399-person metabolomics study found no signal that survived false-discovery-rate correction.[C11,C12,C13] These results demonstrate exposure or generate mechanistic hypotheses. They do not prove deficiency correction, cognitive mediation, slower biological aging, or reduced disease incidence.

## How large was the benefit?

Effects around **0.06–0.07 SD** are small. Small average effects can still matter at population scale if they are durable, inexpensive, safe, and linked to outcomes people feel in daily life. COSMOS has not yet established all of those conditions.

The component studies used different testing modes, outcomes, and follow-up periods. Practice effects were visible in repeated testing, and participants were generally healthy volunteers with limited racial and ethnic diversity. The tested formulation may also matter: COSMOS does not establish a class effect for every multivitamin product.[C03,C05,C06]

The unresolved question is not whether the pooled test-score difference exists. It is whether that difference is **durable, functionally meaningful, generalizable, and capable of changing clinical outcomes**.

## Evidence scorecard

| Domain | Grade | EOS assessment |
|---|---:|---|
| Human randomized evidence | B+ | Large parent trial and multiple randomized ancillary analyses, but cognitive studies share one trial ecosystem and effects are small.[C03,C05,C06,C07] |
| Mechanistic evidence | C | Selected nutrient biomarkers confirm exposure, but no demonstrated pathway explains the cognitive signal or identifies the responsible nutrient.[C11] |
| Safety evidence | B− | No major parent-trial safety signal was established, but fracture estimates leaned weakly adverse (total fracture HR 1.09, 95% CI 1.00–1.19; p=0.059). Fractures were self-reported and not centrally adjudicated, and multiple comparisons were not familywise-adjusted. This is not proof of harm; universal safety cannot be inferred across products, doses, conditions, or drug combinations.[C02,C10] |
| Longevity evidence | D | No demonstrated extension of lifespan, healthspan, independence, or dementia-free survival.[C02,C04] |
| Clinical applicability | C | Evidence supports a small cognitive-test signal, not routine use as established dementia prevention. Individual need and contraindications remain clinical questions.[C04,C06] |

## What we do not know

- Whether the cognitive difference persists after supplementation stops.
- Whether it reduces clinically diagnosed dementia with longer follow-up.
- Whether it preserves daily function or independent living.
- Which nutrient or combination, if any, drives the effect.
- Whether benefit is concentrated among people with specific deficiencies or dietary patterns.
- Whether the result generalizes to other multivitamin formulations.
- Whether similar effects would appear in younger, more diverse, or less-selected populations.
- Which patients, if any, are most likely to experience a clinically meaningful benefit.

## Takeaway

COSMOS supports a **small difference in change on selected cognitive tests** among older adults assigned a daily multivitamin-mineral. The pooled analysis passed its two-outcome correction, but the three studies were nested within one parent trial and were not independent replications.[C03,C05,C06]

The trial did not demonstrate fewer cases of MCI or probable dementia, and its age-equivalence language came from cross-sectional score translations rather than observed delays in clinical decline.[C03,C04,C05,C06]

> **The Insight:** COSMOS detected a small cognitive-test signal. The leap from that signal to “dementia prevention” or “two years younger” is where the evidence runs out.

## Methodology notes

EOS reviewed the primary COSMOS publications, distinguished prespecified from exploratory outcomes, checked multiplicity handling, and compared the findings with prior randomized evidence.

COSMOS combined public funding with commercial support. Mars Edge provided an investigator-initiated grant, infrastructure, and cocoa products; Pfizer Consumer Healthcare provided partial multivitamin/placebo and packaging support. Investigators disclosed institutional or other nutrition-sector relationships. The parent papers report that commercial funders had no role in specified analysis or manuscript decisions. These disclosures matter, but they do not by themselves demonstrate distortion.[C01,C02]
