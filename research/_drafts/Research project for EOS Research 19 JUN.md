I’ve created a research packet for EOS titled **“COSMOS Multivitamin Trial and Cognition: Design, Findings, and Data-Conclusion Alignment”**, focused on what COSMOS tested, what it actually found, and how well public conclusions track the underlying data.[^1][^2]

## What’s in the packet

- **Trial architecture and methods**: Parent COSMOS design (2×2 factorial cocoa extract + multivitamin), cognition sub-studies (COSMOS-Mind, COSMOS-Web, COSMOS-Clinic), and adjudication of MCI/dementia outcomes.[^2][^3][^1]
- **Core cognitive findings**: The multivitamin’s small but statistically significant effects on global cognition and episodic memory, lack of benefit for cocoa extract, and the key result that MCI and dementia incidence were *not* significantly reduced over three years.[^4][^5][^2]
- **Effect size and “cognitive aging” claims**: Walk-through of the z‑score changes and the “≈2 years of cognitive aging slowed” narrative, with an explicit critique of how far that translation can reasonably be pushed clinically.[^6][^4]
- **Strengths, limitations, and mechanistic questions**: Randomization and large N vs. short follow-up, low dementia case counts, measurement constraints (telephone/web vs. in-clinic), and open questions around nutritional status and mechanisms.[^3][^2][^4]

- As a **reference article** for EOS Research when evaluating supplement claims or designing health-related content on cognition and aging.

- As a **template** for future EOS analyses of large trials (clear separation of: what was tested, what was found, and which public conclusions are justified vs. overstated).
