# COSMOS Multivitamin Trial and Cognition: Design, Findings, and Data-Conclusion Alignment

## Executive Summary

The COcoa Supplement and Multivitamin Outcomes Study (COSMOS) is a large, U.S.-based, randomized, placebo-controlled trial testing whether cocoa extract and a standard multivitamin-mineral supplement influence cardiovascular disease, cancer, and cognitive outcomes in older adults. Its cognition program includes three major ancillary studies—COSMOS-Mind (telephone-based), COSMOS-Web (online), and COSMOS-Clinic (in-person)—plus a meta-analysis across these cohorts. Overall, the multivitamin showed small but statistically significant benefits on composite measures of global cognition and episodic memory over approximately 2–3 years, while cocoa extract had no cognitive benefit. However, the data do not demonstrate a reduction in incidence of mild cognitive impairment (MCI) or dementia, and the clinical magnitude of benefit appears modest, raising important questions about how far the conclusions can reasonably be taken.[^1][^2][^3][^4][^5][^6]

This report summarizes what COSMOS tested, what it found for cognitive outcomes (especially MCI and dementia), and critically assesses whether claims that daily multivitamins "slow cognitive aging" or "prevent memory loss" are supported by the data.

## Background and Trial Design

### Parent COSMOS Trial

COSMOS is a large 2 × 2 factorial randomized trial conducted among more than 21,000 generally healthy older adults (≥60 years) across the United States. Participants were randomized to receive cocoa flavanol extract, a daily multivitamin-mineral supplement, both, or double placebo, allowing concurrent evaluation of two interventions without assuming interaction. The parent trial’s primary endpoints were major cardiovascular events and total invasive cancer, and it found no protective effects of either cocoa extract or multivitamin on these primary endpoints.[^7][^5][^6][^1]

### Cognitive Ancillary Studies

Three major ancillary studies examined cognitive outcomes:

- **COSMOS-Mind**: Telephone-based cognitive assessments over three years in approximately 2,262 participants, leveraging standardized batteries for memory, executive function, and global cognition.[^2][^8][^7]
- **COSMOS-Web**: Online, web-based cognitive testing in a separate subset of COSMOS participants (details summarized in the meta-analysis and press release).[^3][^4]
- **COSMOS-Clinic**: In-person neuropsychological testing in a clinic subcohort of about 573 participants, with baseline and two-year follow-up assessments.[^4][^9][^3]

Together, these complementary modes of assessment enable evaluation of multivitamin and cocoa extract effects on cognition across different settings and measurement approaches, while sharing the same underlying randomized structure.[^3][^4]

## Interventions and Population

### Supplements Tested

The interventions included:

- **Multivitamin-mineral supplement**: A commercially available, standard multivitamin containing more than 20 essential micronutrients, provided as a daily pill donated by Pfizer Consumer Healthcare (now Haleon).[^4][^3]
- **Cocoa extract**: A high-flavanol cocoa extract delivering approximately 600 mg/day of cocoa flavanols, encapsulated and taken daily.[^8][^7]

Participants were randomized into four arms:

1. Active multivitamin + active cocoa extract.
2. Active multivitamin + cocoa placebo.
3. Active cocoa extract + multivitamin placebo.
4. Placebo multivitamin + placebo cocoa extract.[^9][^6]

Randomization was stratified by sex, age groups, and recruitment source to balance baseline characteristics.[^9]

### Study Population

COSMOS and its cognition studies enrolled community-dwelling older adults, typically aged 60–80+, with median age around 73 years. Ancillary cohorts had similar demographic patterns, with COSMOS-Mind enrolling 2,282 participants (about 60% women) and COSMOS-Clinic recruiting a subcohort living near the Brigham and Women’s Hospital for detailed in-person evaluations. Participants were generally free of major cognitive impairment at baseline; dementia cases were excluded or identified through screening and adjudication processes.[^2][^7][^8][^9]

## COSMOS-Mind: MCI and Dementia Outcomes

### Study Design

COSMOS-Mind was designed specifically to assess incident mild cognitive impairment (MCI) and probable dementia using annual, telephone-based cognitive testing over three years. It employed a composite primary outcome based on standardized telephone assessments of memory, executive function, and global cognition and used proxy interviews when participants scored below predefined cognitive thresholds to inform adjudication of MCI and dementia outcomes.[^7][^8][^2]

Participants (N = 2,262) underwent baseline and annual cognitive batteries, and incident MCI and dementia were adjudicated using prespecified criteria. The study used proportional hazards regression to compare incidence rates across treatment arms (multivitamin vs. placebo and cocoa extract vs. placebo) under the factorial design.[^2][^7]

### Primary Outcomes: MCI and Dementia Incidence

Over three years of follow-up, COSMOS-Mind adjudicated 110 incident cases of MCI and 14 cases of probable dementia among participants. Analyses demonstrated that incidence rates of MCI and dementia did not differ significantly by assignment to multivitamin-mineral supplementation or cocoa extract; hazard ratios did not show statistically significant reductions and all p-values for incidence comparisons were ≥0.05.[^2]

The authors emphasized that statistical power for dementia incidence was low given the small number of cases, limiting definitive conclusions about dementia risk. Nonetheless, the data provide no evidence of a substantial reduction in incident MCI or dementia over three years attributable to multivitamin or cocoa extract.[^2]

### Secondary Outcomes: Cognitive Performance at Conversion

Although multivitamin-mineral therapy did not significantly reduce the incidence of MCI or dementia, COSMOS-Mind found differences in cognitive trajectories among participants who converted to MCI.[^2]

Key findings:

- When participants assigned to multivitamin-mineral supplementation converted to MCI, their scores for global cognition and executive function at the time of conversion were higher than those of placebo-assigned converters.[^2]
- Their cognitive scores had declined less during the year preceding conversion compared with placebo converters, indicating slower pre-conversion decline.[^2]

Specifically, multivitamin-assigned converters showed statistically significantly higher levels of global cognition (p = 0.03) and executive function (p < 0.001) at conversion, along with smaller year-to-year declines in global cognition (p = 0.03) and executive function (p = 0.004). The authors interpreted these results as evidence that multivitamin therapy may confer "cognitive resilience," reducing the severity of decline leading up to MCI, even though it does not significantly reduce MCI incidence.[^2]

### Cocoa Extract Outcomes

For cocoa extract, COSMOS-Mind found no evidence of beneficial effects on cognitive impairment outcomes.

- Cocoa extract supplementation did not reduce risks for cognitive impairment, including MCI and probable dementia.[^2]
- There were no substantial or consistent benefits on cognitive trajectories compared with placebo.[^7][^2]

Thus, cocoa flavanols, at the tested dose and duration, did not demonstrate cognitive protection in this trial.

## COSMOS-Clinic and COSMOS-Web: Cognitive Function Outcomes

### COSMOS-Clinic (In-Person Assessments)

COSMOS-Clinic was a clinic-based subcohort of approximately 573 participants from the parent trial who lived within driving distance of Brigham and Women’s Hospital. These participants completed detailed health evaluations, including approximately 45-minute neuropsychological assessments at baseline and after two years.[^3][^9]

In COSMOS-Clinic, multivitamin supplementation produced a modest benefit on global cognition over two years compared with placebo. The in-clinic assessments also showed a statistically significant benefit for episodic memory, but not for executive function or attention, suggesting domain-specific effects rather than broad improvements across all cognitive domains.[^4][^3]

Cocoa extract did not show significant benefits in this in-clinic cohort, aligning with the negative cocoa results from COSMOS-Mind.[^9][^3]

### COSMOS-Web (Online Assessments)

COSMOS-Web employed web-based cognitive assessments in another subset of COSMOS participants, providing a complementary mode of data collection and enabling large-scale evaluation of cognitive changes over 2–3 years. Like the other ancillary studies, COSMOS-Web contributed to the broader analysis of multivitamin and cocoa extract effects.[^3][^4]

The web-based study also suggested beneficial effects of daily multivitamin on measures of global cognition and memory, with results consistent in direction with those of COSMOS-Mind and COSMOS-Clinic. However, as with the other cohorts, the magnitude of benefit was modest, and cocoa extract did not demonstrate cognitive advantages.[^4][^3]

## Meta-Analysis of COSMOS Cognition Studies

### Design of the Meta-Analysis

Investigators conducted a prespecified meta-analysis pooling data from the three non-overlapping cognition studies—COSMOS-Mind, COSMOS-Web, and COSMOS-Clinic—covering more than 5,000 participants and 2–3 years of treatment duration.[^3][^4]

The meta-analysis assessed the effect of multivitamin-mineral supplementation versus placebo on global cognition and episodic memory, using standardized mean changes and composite scores to harmonize results across the different testing modalities.[^4][^3]

### Main Meta-Analytic Findings

The meta-analysis found:

- Statistically significant benefits of daily multivitamin supplementation on global cognition and episodic memory across the three studies.[^3][^4]
- These benefits were described as consistent and modest, indicating small improvements relative to placebo but robust across different assessment methods and populations.[^4][^3]

Based on age-related decline patterns observed in the trial, the authors estimated that daily multivitamin supplementation slowed global cognitive aging by the equivalent of about two years compared with placebo. This estimate stems from the relationship between age and composite cognitive scores and the observed difference in cognitive change between multivitamin and placebo groups.[^5][^3]

### Critical Perspective on the Magnitude of Effect

External commentators have noted that the absolute effect size on composite cognitive scores is small.

For example, one analysis highlighted that in COSMOS-Mind, multivitamin supplementation improved the z-score for composite cognition by about 0.07 points at three years, compared with placebo, and that the effect was slightly larger (0.11 points) among participants with a history of cardiovascular disease. Given that composite scores declined by approximately 0.045 points per year with aging, the authors inferred that a 0.07–0.083 point benefit might correspond to approximately 1.8 years of age-related decline offset.[^5]

Critics argue that translating such small changes into "years younger" of cognitive aging may overstate the clinical impact, particularly because the studies did not directly show improved functional outcomes such as reduced dementia incidence or extended independent living. Thus, while the meta-analysis supports a statistically significant benefit on cognitive test scores, the real-world significance is debatable.[^5]

## Alignment of Conclusions with Data

### What the Data Clearly Support

From the COSMOS cognitive program, the following conclusions are well-supported:

1. **Multivitamin-mineral supplementation yields small but statistically significant improvements in global cognition and episodic memory over 2–3 years in older adults.**[^3][^4][^2]
2. **Cocoa extract does not reduce cognitive impairment or improve cognitive trajectories.**[^7][^3][^2]
3. **Multivitamin-mineral supplementation does not significantly reduce incidence of MCI or dementia over three years in the COSMOS-Mind cohort.**[^2]
4. **Among participants who develop MCI, those randomized to multivitamin show higher cognitive scores and less decline at conversion, suggesting some resilience of cognitive function.**[^2]

Collectively, these findings support the idea that multivitamin therapy can slightly improve or preserve cognitive test performance in older adults, but do not establish that it prevents cognitive impairment or dementia within the timeframe studied.[^3][^2]

### Claims That May Overreach the Data

Some communications about COSMOS, including press releases and media coverage, frame the results as showing that daily multivitamins "prevent memory loss" or "slow cognitive aging" in a way that might be interpreted as clinically large or protective against dementia.[^5][^3]

Key tensions between data and broad claims include:

- **Lack of dementia risk reduction:** COSMOS-Mind did not show reduced dementia incidence, and dementia case numbers were limited. Extrapolating from small cognitive test score changes to dementia prevention is not directly supported.[^2]
- **No reduction in MCI incidence:** Multivitamin therapy did not significantly reduce MCI incidence over three years. This challenges strong claims about preventing cognitive impairment.[^2]
- **Small absolute effect sizes:** Composite score differences (e.g., 0.07–0.11 z-score improvement) are statistically significant but small; interpreting them as equivalent to nearly two years of "cognitive aging" may exaggerate practical relevance without corroborating functional outcomes.[^5]
- **Negative primary endpoints for other health outcomes:** The parent COSMOS trial found no cardiovascular or cancer benefit from the multivitamin supplement, undermining mechanistic narratives that improved vascular health explains cognitive benefits.[^1][^5]

Accordingly, the evidence supports modest cognitive test benefits and potential resilience but does not justify broad statements that multivitamins prevent dementia or substantially alter long-term cognitive trajectories.

### Nuanced Interpretation

A nuanced interpretation consistent with the data is:

- Daily multivitamin-mineral supplementation may provide modest improvements in cognitive test performance in older adults and could slightly slow measured decline in global cognition and episodic memory over several years.[^4][^3][^2]
- This benefit appears as small, domain-specific effect sizes rather than dramatic protection against cognitive impairment, and does not translate into significantly fewer cases of MCI or dementia within three years.[^5][^2]
- Cocoa extract, at the dose and duration tested, does not confer cognitive protection in this population.[^3][^2]

Such framing stays within the bounds of observed data and acknowledges both statistical significance and clinical modesty.

## Methodological Strengths and Limitations

### Strengths

Key strengths of the COSMOS cognitive studies include:

- **Randomized, placebo-controlled design:** The factorial randomization ensures balanced comparison groups for multivitamin and cocoa interventions.[^1][^9]
- **Large sample sizes:** Thousands of participants across three ancillary studies improve power to detect modest cognitive changes.[^4][^3]
- **Multiple assessment modalities:** Telephone, web-based, and in-person neuropsychological testing provide diverse and complementary cognitive measures.[^7][^3][^2]
- **Central adjudication of cognitive impairment:** COSMOS-Mind uses standardized criteria and proxy interviews to adjudicate MCI and dementia, enhancing reliability of impairment outcomes.[^7][^2]

These features make COSMOS among the most rigorous large-scale trials testing multivitamins and cocoa extract for cognitive outcomes.

### Limitations

Important limitations temper interpretation:

- **Limited follow-up duration for dementia:** Three years is relatively short for detecting effects on dementia incidence, especially in healthy older adults with low baseline risk.[^5][^2]
- **Low number of dementia cases:** Only 14 dementia cases were adjudicated in COSMOS-Mind, severely limiting statistical power for dementia outcomes.[^2]
- **Telephone and web-based measures:** While validated, remote assessments may be less sensitive to subtle cognitive changes than comprehensive in-person batteries.[^9][^7]
- **Generalizability:** Participants were volunteers in a clinical trial, which may limit applicability to broader, more diverse older populations.[^7]
- **Unclear mechanisms:** The trial did not definitively identify mechanisms through which multivitamins might affect cognition, and improvements could reflect correction of subclinical micronutrient deficits or other factors; mechanistic speculation remains tentative.[^5][^3]

These limitations argue for cautious interpretation of the results and highlight the need for longer-term and mechanistically oriented studies.

## Practical Implications and Open Questions

### For Older Adults and Clinicians

Based on COSMOS, clinicians and older adults may reasonably consider that:

- Daily multivitamin-mineral supplementation is safe and may offer modest cognitive test score benefits, particularly for memory and global cognition, without evidence of major harm.[^3][^2]
- Expectations should remain conservative; multivitamins are not proven to prevent dementia or eliminate the risk of MCI within a few years.[^5][^2]
- Decisions about multivitamin use should also weigh other evidence on micronutrient supplementation, dietary patterns, and overall health, recognizing that many guidelines have not endorsed routine multivitamin use for chronic disease prevention.[^1][^5]

### Open Research Questions

COSMOS raises several unanswered questions:

- Whether longer-term multivitamin use (beyond three years) can meaningfully affect dementia risk.
- How baseline nutritional status modifies cognitive responses to multivitamin supplementation, and whether benefits concentrate among individuals with specific deficiencies.[^3]
- The precise micronutrients or combinations responsible for observed cognitive effects.
- Whether similar benefits would be observed in more diverse populations or those at higher baseline risk for cognitive decline.

Addressing these questions will require extended follow-up, targeted mechanistic studies, and more granular analyses of nutritional status.

## Conclusion

The COSMOS multivitamin and cocoa extract trials provide high-quality evidence about cognitive effects of these supplements in older adults. The data consistently show that daily multivitamin-mineral supplementation produces modest, statistically significant improvements in global cognition and memory over 2–3 years, but does not significantly reduce the incidence of MCI or dementia within the studied timeframe. Cocoa extract fails to show cognitive benefit.[^4][^3][^2]

Consequently, the strongest data-supported conclusion is that multivitamins may slightly enhance or preserve cognitive test performance in older adults, offering potential cognitive resilience but not definitive protection against cognitive impairment. Claims that multivitamins meaningfully prevent dementia or substantially slow cognitive aging should be regarded as extrapolations beyond the available evidence.
